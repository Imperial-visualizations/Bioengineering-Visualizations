Moments of Inertia - Theory

1. Introduction

When a particle or a body enter rotational motion, equivalents can be drawn from linear motion. However, there are some key differences that must
be addressed. For instance, as shown in the first simulation, when the distance from the axis of rotation of a point mass increases while its mass
and kinetic energy remain constant, the tangential velocity of the mass decreases. This implies that for a rigid body or a system of particles rotating
about an axis, all particles have a different tangential velocity, dependent on the radius of its circular path. Therefore, a quantity that relates to the
speed of rotation and is independent of this distance is required, which is known as the angular velocity or frequency, and measured in radians per
second. It is defined in eq.(1), where 'r' is the radius of the circular path and 'T' is the time taken for one complete path (one oscillation).

Torque, or moment of force, is commonly referred to as the rotational analogue of force, being responsible for any angular acceleration. It also shows a
dependency on the distance from the axis of rotation, as displayed in eq.(2) as the cross product of the position and force vectors. Therefore, there is a
quantity that can be treated as the rotational equivalent of mass, representing a body's tendency to resist angular acceleration. This quantity is defined
as the moment of inertia, shown in eq.(3) for a point mass.

$$(1)   \omega={v_t \over r} = {2\pi \over T} $$

$$(2)   {\vec \tau}= {\vec r} \times {\overrightarrow F}$$

$$(3)   I=md^2 $$

2. Rotational Equivalents of Linear Motion

The moment of Inertia is sometimes referred to as the 'rotational equivalent' of mass for linear motion.
It is the sum of the mass of each particle in the body with the square of the distance from the axis of rotation,
As previously mentioned and shown in eq.(3). While mass is also an important component in resisting rotational
motion, just as in linear motion, it also shows a large dependencer on the displacement of each point mass from the
axis of rotation. This can be visually seen through the visualization. When the mass(kg) of one point mass is equal
the distance(m) of the other and vice versa, the heavier one closer to the axis of rotation will have a greater angular
velocity. Furthermore, other rotational quantities also have linear equivalents, as shown in the table to the right.

To quantify the dependence of both mass and distance from the axis of rotation together, the moment
of inertia was defined. For a rigid body (where defomation is negligible) rotating about its center of mass,
the moment of inertia can be found through the sum of the moments of inertia of its infinitely many constituent
point masses. In mathematical terms, this relationship can be represented by the following equation:

$$I_{tot}=\int{r^2.dm} $$
