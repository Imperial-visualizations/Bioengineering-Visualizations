Ampere's law equation:

\(\oint_C\mathbf{B.dl}=\mu_0 \mu_r \sum I\)

Single wire of current


Realize \(\oint_C\mathbf{B.dl} = \mu _0 I.\)
Since the field is constant at a given distance, this gives us: $$\oint_C B.dl = \mu_0 I$$ $$ B\times 2 \pi r = \mu_0 I$$ $$B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} $$ This result could also have been found using Biot-Savart law, but can also be deduced using Ampere's law.

Solenoid inductor

Consider a solenoid inductor, composed of N loops of current-carrying wire, which can be considered infinitely long except at the edges.
The magnetic field inside the solenoid can be found by painfully applying Biot-Savart law or by simple application of Ampere's law.

\(\mathbf{B}\) inside the solenoid can be considered as constant, 0 outside of the rings and \(\mathbf{B.dl}=0\) on the perpendicular trajectories. The only part contributing to the loop integral is the one inside the solenoid.
On that path, \(\mathbf{B.dl}=|B||dl|\). Therefore: $$\oint_C \mathbf{B.dl}=\oint_C B\, dl = \mu_0 NI $$ $$B\times L = \mu_0 NI$$ $$B = \frac{\mu_0 NI}{L} = n\mu_0I$$

Toroid inductor



A toroidal inductor is composed of loops of current wrapped in a donut shape - ie a wire wound around a ring N times, carrying current I.

For C1, \(\oint \mathbf{B_1.dl}=0\). There is no current comprised inside the loop.

For C3, \(\oint \mathbf{B_3.dl}=\). Inside the loop, there are as many current wires carrying current out of the page as towards the page.

For C2, a loop passing through the center of the toroid, \(\oint_{C_2}\mathbf{B_2.dl}=\mu_0 N I\). Since there are N wires carrying current I into the page and no current going out: \(\sum\limits^N{I}=N\times I\). If the radius of \(C_2\) is \(r_2\):

$$\oint \mathbf{B_2.dl}=2\pi r_2\times B_2= \mu_0 N I$$ $$B_2=\frac{\mu_0 NI}{2\pi r_2}$$
